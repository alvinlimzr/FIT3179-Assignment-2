Health & Wealth: a short data story

Three concise visuals explore how GDP per capita relates to life expectancy. Start with a global snapshot, then examine the overall relationship, and finally trace how a few countries moved through this space since 1960.

1) Global snapshot — World GDP per capita (2024)

What to notice: High-income clusters in North America, Western Europe and Oceania; gaps across parts of Africa and South Asia. Use this as context before we relate income to health.

2) Relationship — Life expectancy vs GDP per capita

The curve flattens: early income gains accompany large health improvements, but at high incomes, each extra dollar buys fewer years. Look for regional outliers (e.g., countries with higher life expectancy than peers at similar income levels).

Tip: Watch IND, CHN, USA, SGP across years.

3) Country paths — how nations moved from 1960 to today

Why this view: A connected-scatter reveals the path countries take, not just their position. Notice the different slopes: fast GDP growth with rapid health gains (Singapore, China) versus wealth without commensurate life-expectancy improvement (United States).

Takeaways

  • Income strongly relates to longevity, but diminishing returns set in at higher GDP per capita.
  • Policy and health systems matter: at similar incomes, outcomes can differ markedly.
  • Development paths vary—some countries convert growth into health faster than others.

Sources: World Bank (GDP per capita, current US$); Our World in Data / UN (life expectancy). Notes: GDP snaps to nearest available year in the data; countries with missing values are excluded.
